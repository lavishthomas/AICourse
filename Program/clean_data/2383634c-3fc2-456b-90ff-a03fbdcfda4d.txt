Why do you need to index your site?

First, the obvious answer.

If you want your site to show up in the search results at all, then it needs to be indexed.

However, you don‚Äôt want your site to be indexed just once. You want the search engines to keep re-indexing your site.

Search engines like Google don‚Äôt just update automatically.

They rely on spiders ‚Äî little bits of computer code that each search engine sends out to ‚Äúcrawl‚Äù the web (hence, ‚Äúspider‚Äù). You want an efficient, frequent crawl rate.

The spider‚Äôs job is to look for new stuff on the web and update the already indexed version of your site. That ‚Äúnew stuff‚Äù can be a new page on an existing site, a change to an existing page, or an entirely new site or blog.

Once the spider finds a new site or page, it needs to figure out what that new site or page is about.

Way back in the Wild Wild West of the early web, search engine spiders weren‚Äôt nearly as smart as they are today. You could force a spider to index and rank your page based on nothing more than how many times a particular search phrase (‚Äúkeyword‚Äù) appeared on the page.

For today‚Äôs content success, you can‚Äôt rely on these old school search engine optimization strategies.

And the keyword didn‚Äôt even have to be in the body of the page itself. Many people ranked for their biggest competitor‚Äôs brand name just by stuffing dozens of variations of that brand name in a page‚Äôs meta tags!

Fortunately for Google search users and ethical website owners, those days are long gone.

Today, keyword and meta tag stuffing will get you penalized, not rewarded. And meta keyword tags aren‚Äôt really part of the algorithm at all (though there are still good reasons to use them ).

If you‚Äôre not careful, you could get your site kicked out of the index altogether ‚Äî which means your site won‚Äôt rank for any keywords at all.

These days, Google is much more concerned with the overall user experience on your site and the user intention behind the search ‚Äî i.e., does the user want to buy something (commercial intent) or learn something (informational intent)?

Don‚Äôt get me wrong ‚Äî keywords still matter. Other factors are also important ‚Äî up to 200 altogether, according to Brian Dean of Backlinko. These include things like quality inbound links, social signals (though not directly), and valid code on all your pages.

But none of that will matter if the spiders can‚Äôt tell the search engines your pages are there in the first place, meaning they won‚Äôt show up in search results.

And that‚Äôs why indexing is so important.

To put it simply, indexing is the spider‚Äôs way of gathering and processing all the data from pages and sites during its crawl around the web.

Frequent indexing improves your search results.

The spider notes new documents and changes, which are then added to the searchable index Google maintains. Those pages are only added if they contain quality content and don‚Äôt trigger any alarms by doing shady things like keyword stuffing or building a bunch of links from unreputable sources.

When the spider sees a change on your website, it processes both the content (text) on the page as well as the locations on the page where search terms are placed. It also analyzes the titles tag, meta tag, and alt attributes for images.

That spider then adds, or ‚Äúindexes‚Äù, that content into Google.

That‚Äôs indexing in a nutshell. It is an essential webmaster tool.

When a search user comes along looking for information by typing in search keywords, Google‚Äôs algorithm goes to work. The algorithm then decides where to rank a page in comparison to all the other pages related to those keywords.

How often your site is indexed can affect your performance in search results. You want to make sure all your latest content is available for those searching and Google‚Äôs spiders at all times.

That‚Äôs the short and somewhat simplified version of how Google finds, analyzes, and indexes new sites like yours. Many other search engines, like Bing or Yahoo, follow similar procedures, though there can be variations in the specifics as each has its own algorithm.

Step 7: Add a sitemap plugin to get Google to index your site

First, let‚Äôs talk about what a sitemap is.

You‚Äôve undoubtedly seen the word ‚Äúsitemap‚Äù before ‚Äì but maybe you never knew exactly what it meant and how it relates to search engine optimization. Here‚Äôs the definition Google pulls for us:

The sitemap is basically a list (in XML format) of all the pages on your site. Its primary function is to let search engines know when something‚Äôs changed ‚Äì either a new web page, or changes on a specific page ‚Äì as well as how often the search engine should check for changes.

Do sitemaps affect your search rankings ? Probably not ‚Äì at least, not significantly. But they will help your site get indexed more quickly with a more efficient crawl rate.

Image from FirstPageSage.com

In today‚Äôs Hummingbird-driven world of search , there are a lot of SEO myths you need to be wary of. But one thing remains the same: all things being equal, great content will rise to the top, just like cream.

Sitemaps help your content get crawled and indexed so it can rise to the top of SERPs more quickly, according to the Google Webmaster Blog . In Google‚Äôs own words, ‚ÄúSubmitting a Sitemap helps you make sure Google knows about the URLs on your site.‚Äù

Is it a guarantee your site will be indexed immediately? No. But it is definitely an effective webmaster tool that helps in that process.

Casey Henry wondered just how much sitemaps would impact crawl rate and indexing , so he decided to conduct a little experiment.

Casey talked to one of his clients who ran a fairly popular blog using both WordPress and the Google XML Sitemaps Generator WordPress plugin (more on that below).

With the client‚Äôs permission, Casey installed a tracking script, which would track the actions of Googlebot on the site. It also tracked when the bot accessed the sitemap, when the sitemap was submitted, and each page that was crawled. This data was stored in a database along with a timestamp, IP address, and the user agent.

The client just continued his regular posting schedule (about two or three posts each week).

Step 10: Use robots.txt

If you‚Äôre not an expert coder or developer, you might have seen a file called ‚Äúrobots.txt‚Äù in your domain‚Äôs files and wondered what it is and what it does .

The ‚Äúwhat it is‚Äù part is very simple. It‚Äôs a basic, plain text file that should reside in the root directory of your domain. If you‚Äôre using WordPress, it‚Äôll be in the root directory of your WordPress installation.

The ‚Äúwhat it does‚Äù part is a little more complex. Basically, robots.txt is a file that gives strict instructions to search engine bots about which pages they can crawl and index ‚Äî and which pages to stay away from.

When search spiders find this file on a new domain, they read the instructions in it before doing anything else. If they don‚Äôt find a robots.txt file, the search bots assume that you want every page crawled and indexed.

Now you might wonder ‚ÄúWhy on earth would I want search engines not to index a page on my site?‚Äù That‚Äôs a good question!

In short, it‚Äôs because not every page that exists on your site should be counted as a separate page for search result purposes. More on this topic later!

Your first step is to confirm that your new site has a robots.txt file. You can do this either by FTP or by clicking on your File Manager via CPanel (or the equivalent, if your hosting company doesn‚Äôt use CPanel).

If it‚Äôs not there, you can create one quite easily using a plain text editor like Notepad.

Note: It‚Äôs very important to use only a plain text editor, and not something like Word or WordPad, which can insert invisible codes into your document that will really mess things up.

WordPress bloggers can optimize their robots.txt files by using a reliable WordPress plugin like Yoast‚Äôs SEO plugin.

The format of a robots.txt file is pretty simple. The first line usually names a user agent, which is just the name of the search bot ‚Äì e.g., Googlebot or Bingbot. You can also use an asterisk (*) as a wildcard identifier for all bots. This type of WordPress plugin is an effective webmaster tool.

Next is a string of Allow or Disallow commands. These tell search engines which parts they should crawl and index (‚ÄúAllow‚Äù) and which parts they should ignore (‚ÄúDisallow‚Äù).

These rules ensure that only the pages you want to get indexed end up in search results.

So to recap: the function of robots.txt is to tell search engines what to do with the content/pages on your site. But does it help get your site indexed?

Harsh Agrawal of ShoutDreams Media says

Yes.

He was able to get sites indexed within 24 hours using a combination of strategies, including robots.txt and on-page SEO techniques.

All that being said, it‚Äôs crucial to be very cautious when revising your robots.txt file, because it‚Äôs easy to make a mistake if you don‚Äôt know what you‚Äôre doing.

An incorrectly configured file can hide your entire site from search engines. This is the exact opposite of what you want! You must understand how to edit your robots.txt file properly to prevent hurting your crawl rate.

There are some pages you don‚Äôt want Google or other search engines to index. Here are the pages you don‚Äôt want to show up in search:

Option 1: In your robots.txt file

Remember that robots.txt file we made back in Step 10? You can add directives in it to tell search engines not to index a file, or a whole directory. That can be handy when you want to make sure an entire section of your site remains unindexed.

First, let‚Äôs cover what they mean.

Nofollow: Tells Google not to crawl the links on that page.

So what that means is if I link over to your website from neilpatel.com and use a nofollow link, none of my link juice (or, domain authority) will be passed over to you.

Noindex: Tells Google not to index the page, even though the spider can see it. It won‚Äôt be added to search results.

You can use both at the same time.

Here‚Äôs an example.

If you‚Äôre linking over to a special landing page for an event you‚Äôre having for VIPs only, and don‚Äôt want that page to appear in search results, you would link to it with a noindex tag.

Even if people search for ‚ÄúNeil‚Äôs super cool event‚Äù in Google, that page won‚Äôt show up.

You will, however, get a lot of Neil Diamond tribute concert options.

Most often, you‚Äôll want to use the noindex tag. You usually only want to use nofollow for affiliate links, links someone has paid you to create, or you receive a commission from. This is because you don‚Äôt want to ‚Äúsell links‚Äù. When you add nofollow, it tells Google not to pass on your domain authority to those sources. Essentially, it keeps the web free of corruption when it comes to linking.
